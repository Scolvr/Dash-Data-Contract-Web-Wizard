Why Token Naming Matters

Your token's name is the first thing users see and forms the foundation of your token's brand identity. On Dash Platform, the name is stored on-chain as part of your token contract and becomes a permanent part of your token's identity.

Key considerations:

  • Permanence: Name changes require governance approval via conventionsChangeRules (not freely mutable)
  • Visibility: Appears in wallets, explorers, and all user interfaces
  • Localization: English ('en') is mandatory; other languages are optional

  • Uniqueness: Should be distinct from other tokens to avoid confusion

Step-by-Step: Naming Your Token

Step 1: Brainstorm Names

Consider your token's purpose and target audience. Ask yourself:

  • What problem does this token solve?
  • Who will use this token?
  • What feeling or association should it evoke?
  • How will it be used in everyday conversation?

Step 2: Check Technical Requirements

Ensure your chosen name meets Dash Platform v2.0 requirements:

  • Token name: 2-64 characters
  • Singular and plural forms: 3-25 characters each (both required)
  • Language codes: ISO 639-1 standard (2-letter codes like 'en', 'es', 'fr')
  • English localization is mandatory; other languages are optional
  • Use standard characters (letters, numbers, spaces)
  • Avoid excessive special characters for better compatibility

Step 3: Define Plural Form

The plural form is how your token appears when displaying quantities greater than 1:

  • "You have 100 Tokens" (not "100 Token")
  • Can be different from just adding 's' (e.g., "Berry" → "Berries")
  • Must be 3-25 characters (same as singular form)
  • Should sound natural in context
  • Test the plural form in example sentences before finalizing

Step 4: Configure Capitalization

Enable capitalization if:

  • Your token name is a proper noun (brand name)
  • You want consistent uppercase presentation
  • Example: "dash" becomes "Dash"

Leave disabled if:

  • You want lowercase flexibility ("credit" or "Credit" depending on context)
  • Your token represents a generic unit

Quick Reference

Important: Token names are NOT freely mutable on Dash Platform. All name changes must be approved through the conventionsChangeRules governance system you configure here.

Choose who can approve name changes:

  • Contract Owner (you) - Only you can approve changes
  • Specific Identity (ID-based) - Delegate approval to another trusted identity
  • Group (shared control) - Multi-signature governance requiring committee approval
  • No One (fully locked) - Names are permanently locked and can never be changed

What This Controls

The conventionsChangeRules determine whether your token's display conventions can be updated after deployment, including:

  • Token name (singular and plural forms in all languages)
  • Localization strings (ISO 639-1 translations)
  • Capitalization preferences (shouldCapitalize boolean)
  • Display decimals (in some configurations)

Platform validation: Dash Platform v2.0 validates all name changes against the 3-25 character limit for singular/plural forms.

Best Practices

  • Early-stage projects: Enable changes with Owner or Group control for flexibility during development and rebranding.
  • Mature projects: Lock changes (set to "No One") after establishing brand identity and exchange listings to build trust.
  • DAO tokens: Use Group control for community governance over all naming decisions.
  • Enterprise tokens: Assign to Specific Identity for compliance or regulatory control.
  • Test on Platform v2.0 testnet: Validate governance workflows before mainnet deployment.

Critical Warnings

Permanent Lock: Setting this to "No One" is irreversible and cannot be undone. Your token's name will be permanently locked forever.

Rebranding Considerations: If you anticipate potential rebranding or name corrections, keep this permission enabled with appropriate governance controls.

Governance Requirement: Remember that changes are not freely mutable - they always require approval through conventionsChangeRules, even if enabled.

Comprehensive guide to configuring conventionsChangeRules governance on Dash Platform.

Overview

The conventionsChangeRules setting determines who can modify your token's display conventions after deployment. This is a critical governance decision that affects your token's long-term flexibility and trustworthiness.

Key Concept: Token names on Dash Platform are NOT freely mutable. All changes require approval through the governance system you configure here, even when changes are enabled.

Governance Permission Models

Contract Owner (Centralized Control)

Who controls it: Only you (the contract owner) can approve name changes.

Best for: Early-stage or centrally managed tokens where quick decisions are needed.

Risks: Single point of control; users must trust the owner completely.

Example use case: Internal company token, beta projects, personal tokens.

Specific Identity (Delegated Control)

Who controls it: A specific Dash Platform identity you designate (ID-based).

Best for: Managed services, regulatory compliance, or trusted third-party administration.

Risks: Still centralized; requires trust in the designated identity.

Example use case: Enterprise tokens managed by compliance department, tokens administered by a foundation.

Group (Multi-Signature Governance)

Who controls it: A committee requiring multiple approvals (multi-sig).

Best for: DAOs, community-governed tokens, and projects requiring distributed trust.

Benefits: No single point of failure; transparent decision-making; community involvement.

Example use case: DAO governance tokens, community projects, tokens with elected committees.

No One (Permanent Lock)

Who controls it: No one - names are permanently immutable.

Best for: Established brands, exchange-listed tokens, maximum trust scenarios.

Warning: This is irreversible. Once locked, names can NEVER be changed by anyone.

Example use case: Mature tokens on exchanges, stablecoins, tokens with established brand identity.

Platform Requirements

  • All name changes are validated by Dash Platform v2.0
  • Singular and plural forms must remain 3-25 characters
  • Language codes must remain ISO 639-1 compliant (2-letter codes)
  • English ('en') localization remains mandatory even in updates
  • Changes go through governance approval workflow (not instant)

Real-World Governance Evolution

Scenario 1: Startup Token Lifecycle

  • Phase 1 (Beta): Owner control - rapid iteration and branding changes
  • Phase 2 (Growth): Transition to Group control as community grows
  • Phase 3 (Mature): Lock permanently when listing on major exchanges

Scenario 2: DAO Token from Day One

  • Launch: Start with Group control for community governance
  • Forever: Maintain Group control to ensure decentralized decision-making
  • Option: Community can vote to lock permanently if desired

Scenario 3: Stablecoin or Brand Token

  • Launch: Lock immediately (No One) to establish maximum trust
  • Benefit: Users know the name can never change
  • Trade-off: Zero flexibility for rebranding or corrections

Decision Framework

Ask yourself:

  • Is my token name final, or might it need adjustments?
  • Do I need centralized control or community governance?
  • Will my token be listed on exchanges (requires stability)?
  • Is building trust through immutability more important than flexibility?
  • Do I have a governance committee or DAO structure?

What it is: Initial quantity of tokens created at launch (immutable after deployment).

Immutability: Base supply cannot be changed after deployment - choose carefully

Important constraint: Cannot exceed this limit even with minting enabled

Use case: Enable to create scarcity and build investor confidence (like Bitcoin's 21M cap)

Changing Max Supply

What it controls: Whether authorized users can modify the max supply cap after deployment.

Enable changes if: Your tokenomics may need future adjustments or flexibility

Disable changes if: You want a permanently fixed hard cap (like Bitcoin's immutable 21M)

Token Supply - Complete Guide

Comprehensive guide to configuring your token's supply parameters: decimals, base supply, max supply, and governance.

Understanding Token Supply

This step establishes the fundamental economic parameters of your token. These settings determine divisibility and supply limits that shape your token's scarcity and value throughout its entire lifecycle.

Why supply configuration matters:

  • Supply determines scarcity: Fixed vs. flexible supply affects token value perception and investor confidence
  • Decimals affect usability: Too few limits micro-transactions, too many adds unnecessary complexity
  • Base supply is immutable: You cannot change initial supply after deployment, so choose carefully
  • Max supply controls inflation: Hard caps prevent unlimited token creation and establish trust

Key principle: Keep supply configuration simple and focused. Advanced features like history tracking, freezing, and emergency pause are configured in separate dedicated screens later in the wizard.

Step-by-Step Configuration

Step 1: Choose Decimal Precision

Decimals determine the smallest unit of your token. Dash Platform supports 0-16 decimal places (default: 8).

Decimal ranges and use cases:

  • 0 decimals: Whole numbers only - tickets, vouchers, NFTs, collectibles
  • 2 decimals: Like dollars and cents (1.00, 2.50) - loyalty points, store credits
  • 6 decimals: Moderate precision - reward points, game currencies, internal credits
  • 8 decimals: Bitcoin-style (0.00000001 = 1 satoshi) - default, good balance for most tokens
  • 16 decimals: Maximum precision allowed by Dash Platform - high-precision financial tokens

Platform constraint: Maximum 16 decimal places on Dash Platform (not 18 like Ethereum).

Immutability: Decimal precision CANNOT be changed after deployment. Choose carefully based on your use case.

Example: With 2 decimals, smallest unit is 0.01. With 8 decimals, it's 0.00000001 (1 satoshi)

Step 2: Set Base Supply (Initial Supply)

Base supply is the initial quantity of tokens created at launch. This value is immutable and cannot be changed after deployment.

Default: 100,000 tokens

Planning considerations:

  • Consider your total addressable market and user base
  • Plan for distribution allocation: team, investors, community, treasury reserves
  • Account for decimal places in calculations (1,000,000 with 8 decimals = 1,000,000.00000000)
  • Leave room for future minting if your token needs inflation (set base supply lower than max supply)

Platform constraint: Base supply is immutable after deployment - you cannot increase or decrease this initial amount.

Common approach: Start with 10%-50% of max supply as base supply, reserving the rest for future minting through controlled emission schedules.

Example: Base supply 1,000,000 + Max supply 10,000,000 = room to mint 9,000,000 more tokens over time

Step 3: Define Max Supply (Hard Cap)

Max supply is the maximum number of tokens that can ever exist. This creates a hard cap on total supply and can be modified through governance (if changes are enabled).

Platform constraints:

  • Maximum value: 2^64 - 1 (18,446,744,073,709,551,615)
  • If max supply equals base supply, minting is permanently disabled
  • Max supply can be modified post-deployment via maxSupplyChangeRules governance

Enable max supply if:

  • You want a hard cap to create scarcity (like Bitcoin's 21 million)
  • Your tokenomics requires predictable supply limits for investor confidence
  • You want to prevent infinite inflation and establish trust
  • Token will be listed on exchanges (fixed caps are often required)

Disable max supply (no cap) if:

  • You need maximum flexibility for future growth and scaling
  • Your token represents ongoing rewards/services with no natural limit
  • Supply adjusts dynamically based on demand (algorithmic/elastic tokens)
  • Token is for internal use without external trading

Important decision: Having a max supply builds trust and scarcity. Not having one provides flexibility but may reduce investor confidence.

Step 4: Configure Max Supply Change Rules

If you enabled max supply, decide whether the cap can be changed after deployment. This is controlled through the maxSupplyChangeRules governance system.

Allow changes if:

  • Your tokenomics may evolve (e.g., increase cap if adoption exceeds projections)
  • You want flexibility to adjust supply based on market conditions
  • Governance committee or DAO will make cap decisions democratically
  • Token is in early stages and parameters may need adjustment

Disable changes if:

  • You want absolute certainty and transparency about the hard cap
  • Immutability is a core value proposition (like Bitcoin's 21M)
  • Token will be listed on major exchanges requiring fixed supply
  • Your tokenomics whitepaper promises a permanent cap

Governance safeguards (advanced):

If you allow changes, you can also enable governance safeguards that let you permanently lock the max supply in the future:

  • Allow changing authorized changer to "No One": Enables permanently removing all ability to change max supply later (use case: "adjustable now, lockable permanently once distribution completes")
  • Allow changing rule changer to "No One": Rule changers can vote to lock themselves out, making rules immutable (use case: "committee can self-lock after achieving stable tokenomics")
  • Allow rule changer to modify themselves: Enables transferring control to different identities/groups (use case: "owner can hand over max supply control to DAO governance later")

Recommendation: Keep all safeguards disabled (default) unless you specifically need the option to permanently lock max supply in the future. Most tokens don't need this complexity.

Common Supply Mistakes

Mistake 1: Too many decimals for simple tokens

Don't use 16 decimals for loyalty points or tickets. Use 0-2 instead. Remember: Dash Platform max is 16 decimals (not 18 like Ethereum).

Mistake 2: Wrong base supply calculation

Remember: 1,000,000 tokens with 8 decimals = 1,000,000.00000000 actual units = 100,000,000,000,000 base units (satoshis). Always account for decimals.

Mistake 3: Setting max supply equal to base supply unintentionally

Manual Freeze Permission - Complete Guide

Comprehensive guide to configuring freeze, unfreeze, and destroy frozen funds functionality for compliance and security.

Understanding Freeze Functionality

Manual freeze gives authorized users the ability to temporarily lock specific token balances, preventing transfers while preserving balance visibility. This is essential for compliance, security, and regulatory requirements.

Key Concepts:

  • Freezing: Locks a balance so tokens cannot be transferred out
  • Unfreezing: Restores frozen balances to normal operational state
  • Destroy Frozen Funds: Permanently removes tokens from frozen accounts (compliance feature)
  • Allow Transfer to Frozen: Lets users send tokens to frozen accounts (receiver can't spend until unfrozen)

Default State: All freeze functionality is disabled (NoOne authorization) by default to maximize decentralization.

Freezing Configuration

When to Enable Freezing

Enable freezing for:

  • Regulated tokens: Stablecoins, securities, or tokens subject to AML/KYC requirements
  • Security response: Need ability to freeze compromised accounts
  • Compliance workflows: Court orders, fraud investigations, regulatory actions
  • Enterprise tokens: Internal tokens requiring policy enforcement

Keep disabled (NoOne) for:

  • Fully decentralized tokens: Maximum censorship resistance
  • Community tokens: Where freeze authority conflicts with decentralization goals
  • Simple use cases: Tokens that don't need compliance features

Authorization Levels

Choose who can freeze balances:

  • NoOne: Freezing permanently disabled (default)
  • ContractOwner: Only the token creator can freeze
  • Identity: Specific Dash Platform identity (e.g., compliance officer ID)
  • MainGroup: Primary governance group (multi-sig)
  • Group: Custom group (max 256 members, voting power max 65,536)

Recommendation: Use Group authorization for compliance tokens to distribute trust and prevent single point of control.

Unfreezing Configuration

Why Unfreeze Matters

Unfreezing allows frozen balances to be restored to normal operation. You can configure unfreezing independently from freezing to create flexible compliance workflows.

Common Patterns:

  • Same authority as freeze: Whoever can freeze can also unfreeze (most common)
  • Different authority: Freeze controlled by security team, unfreeze by compliance committee
  • Higher authority for unfreeze: Easy to freeze (single actor), hard to unfreeze (multi-sig)
  • Permanent freeze: Enable freezing but disable unfreezing (NoOne) for one-way locks

Unfreeze Authorization Options

Same authorization levels as freeze:

  • NoOne: Once frozen, always frozen (permanent lock)
  • ContractOwner: Only owner can unfreeze
  • Identity: Specific identity controls unfreezing
  • MainGroup/Group: Multi-sig approval required to unfreeze

Real-World Use Cases

Pattern 1: Balanced Compliance (Recommended)

  • • Unfreeze: Group (same committee)
  • • Result: Distributed trust, reversible freezes

  • • Unfreeze: NoOne (disabled)

Destroy Frozen Funds

What It Does

Destroy frozen funds permanently removes tokens from frozen accounts, reducing total supply. This is an advanced compliance feature for severe regulatory actions.

When to enable:

  • Regulatory compliance: Court orders requiring permanent seizure
  • Fraud response: Removing stolen/fraudulent tokens from circulation
  • Sanctions compliance: Permanently destroying tokens in sanctioned accounts
  • Legal requirements: Jurisdictions requiring token destruction capability

When to disable (NoOne):

  • Most tokens: This is an extreme measure rarely needed
  • Decentralized projects: Conflicts with censorship resistance
  • Community tokens: Too much centralized control

Authorization Strategy

Recommended approach: If enabling, use highest level of authorization (Group with large committee) to ensure this power is not abused.

Best practice: Require higher authority for destroying than freezing:

  • • Freeze: Identity (compliance officer)
  • • Unfreeze: Group (compliance committee)
  • • Destroy Frozen: MainGroup (executive board + compliance + legal)

Key constraint: You can only destroy tokens from accounts that are currently frozen. Freeze must be enabled for destroy frozen funds to work.

Allow Transfer to Frozen Balances

What It Does

When enabled, users can send tokens TO frozen accounts (even though the frozen account cannot spend them). The receiver's balance increases, but they cannot transfer tokens out until unfrozen.

Use cases for enabling:

  • Escrow systems: Send payment to frozen account, unfreeze when conditions are met
  • Refunds to frozen accounts: Return tokens to users whose accounts are frozen pending investigation
  • Payroll during investigation: Continue paying employee while account is under review
  • Compliance workflows: Separate freezing from fund recovery

Use cases for disabling (default):

  • Simple freeze logic: Frozen means completely isolated
  • Security concern: Prevent accidental transfers to frozen accounts
  • Cleaner UX: Users can't send to frozen accounts (transaction fails early)

Practical Example

Scenario: Fraud investigation with refund capability

  • 1. User account suspected of fraud → freeze enabled
  • 2. Investigation determines some transactions were legitimate refunds
  • 3. Because allowTransferToFrozenBalance is enabled, refunds can be sent to frozen account
  • 4. After investigation, account is unfrozen and user can access all funds

Without this feature, refunds would fail and require manual processing after unfreezing.

Platform behavior: This is a boolean flag (true/false), not an authorization level. Either all users can send to frozen accounts, or no one can.

What is Perpetual Distribution?

Ecosystem Incentives: Create ongoing rewards for developers, content creators, or community contributors.

Distribution Cadence Types

Block-based: Release tokens every N blocks. Predictable based on blockchain activity (e.g., every 1000 blocks ≈ 2.5 hours on Dash).

Time-based: Release tokens at specific time intervals (hours, days, weeks). More intuitive for users but may vary based on actual block times.

Epoch-based: Release tokens at defined epoch boundaries (~18 days per epoch on Dash Platform). Useful for coordinating with governance or protocol events.

Emission Functions (9 Official Types)

Dash Platform supports 9 emission function types. All functions support optional min_value and max_value bounds:

1. FixedAmount: Constant tokens per period (f(x) = amount)

2. Random: Uniform distribution between min and max values

3. StepDecreasingAmount: Discrete reductions over time (like Bitcoin halvings)

4. Linear: f(x) = (a*(x-s)/d) + b with fractional slope for gradual changes

5. Polynomial: Supports fractional exponents for square roots, cube roots, or power functions

6. Exponential: Rapid growth or decay (f(x) = a*base^((m/n)*x) / d)

7. Logarithmic: Slowing growth over time (f(x) = a*log(x) + b)

8. InvertedLogarithmic: Front-loaded emissions with gradual decline

9. Stepwise: Constant values across specific intervals with custom amounts

Emission Functions - Complete Reference

Dash Platform supports 9 official emission function types. All functions (except Random) support optional min_value and max_value bounds to constrain emissions within a range.

1. FixedAmount

Formula: f(x) = amount

Description: Releases the same constant amount every period. Simplest and most predictable emission model.

Use case: Stable staking rewards, consistent mining rewards

Parameters: amount (u64)

Optional bounds: min_value, max_value

2. Random

Formula: f(x) = uniform_random(min, max)

Description: Releases a random amount between min and max using uniform distribution. Creates unpredictability and gamification.

Use case: Gamified rewards, lottery-style distributions

Parameters: min (u64), max (u64) - both required

3. StepDecreasingAmount

Formula: Decreases by factor (numerator/denominator) every step_count intervals

Description: Discrete reductions at specified intervals. Classic halving mechanism like Bitcoin (1/2 every 4 years).

Use case: Bitcoin-style halvings, phased reward reductions

Parameters: step_count, numerator, denominator, offset (optional), initial_amount, trailing_amount, max_interval_count

Example: Halving every 210,000 blocks: step_count=210000, numerator=1, denominator=2

Optional bounds: min_value

4. Linear

Formula: f(x) = (a * (x - s) / d) + b

Description: Linear change with fractional slope (a/d). Emissions increase or decrease gradually over time.

Use case: Gradual emission ramps, controlled inflation/deflation

Parameters: a (slope numerator, i64), d (slope divisor, u64), s (start step, optional i64), b (starting amount, i64)

Example: Start at 1000, decrease by 10 per period: a=-10, d=1, s=0, b=1000

Optional bounds: min_value, max_value

5. Polynomial

Formula: f(x) = (a * x^(m/n) / d) + o + b

Description: Supports fractional exponents for square roots (1/2), cube roots (1/3), or any power function. Advanced mathematical curves.

Use case: Complex emission curves, square root diminishing returns

Parameters: a (scaling factor, i64), m (exponent numerator, i8), n (exponent denominator, u8), d (divisor, u64), s (start offset, optional u64), o (offset, i64), b (base amount, u64)

Example: Square root emission: m=1, n=2 creates f(x) = √x curve

Optional bounds: min_value, max_value

6. Exponential

Formula: f(x) = (a * base^((m/n) * (x - s)) / d) + o + b

Description: Rapid growth (m/n > 0) or decay (m/n < 0). Most aggressive emission curves.

Use case: Exponential decay for long-tail emissions, aggressive early rewards

Parameters: a (scaling factor, u64), m (exponent rate numerator, i8), n (exponent rate denominator, u8), d (divisor, u64), s (start period offset, optional u64), o (offset, i64), b (base amount, u64)

Example: Exponential decay: m=-1, n=2 creates half-life decay

Optional bounds: min_value, max_value

7. Logarithmic

Formula: f(x) = (a * log(x - s) / d) + o + b

Description: Slowing growth over time. Emissions increase but at diminishing rate as time progresses.

Use case: Long-term sustainable growth, diminishing rewards with time

Parameters: a (scaling factor, i64), d (divisor, u64), s (start offset, optional u64), o (offset, i64), b (base amount, u64)

Example: Logarithmic growth: a=100, d=1, creates slow increasing rewards

Optional bounds: min_value, max_value

8. InvertedLogarithmic

Formula: f(x) = (a * log(max_x - (x - s)) / d) + o + b

Description: Front-loaded emissions that decline over time (inverse of logarithmic). High initial rewards that taper off.

Use case: Bootstrap liquidity, early adopter incentives

Parameters: a (scaling factor, i64), d (divisor, u64), s (start offset, optional u64), max_x (maximum x value, u64), o (offset, i64), b (base amount, u64)

Example: High early rewards that decrease: front-loads emissions to early participants

Optional bounds: min_value, max_value

9. Stepwise

Formula: f(x) = defined_amount[interval]

Description: Custom amounts at specific intervals. Complete control over exact emissions at each interval.

Use case: Custom emission schedules, phase-based rewards (Year 1: 1000, Year 2: 750, Year 3: 500)

Parameters: Array of (interval, amount) pairs

Example: [(0, 1000), (365, 750), (730, 500)] for annual changes

No bounds: Already explicitly defined per interval

  • max_value (u64): Caps emissions at this amount, even if formula would produce higher value
  • Use case: Prevent extreme values, guarantee minimum rewards, cap inflation rate

What is an Emission Function?

Fixed Amount

Formula: Same constant amount every period (e.g., 100 tokens per release).

Best for: Simple, predictable reward systems where consistency is valued. Easy for users to understand and calculate expected returns.

Characteristics: Linear supply growth, constant absolute inflation (decreasing percentage inflation over time as total supply grows).

Exponential Decay

Formula: Amount decreases by a percentage over time (similar to Bitcoin halving: a × (m/n)^(period/d)).

Best for: Creating scarcity over time, mimicking mining economics, rewarding early adopters while extending emission lifetime.

Characteristics: Rapidly decreasing emissions, asymptotic approach to maximum supply, strong early-adopter incentives.

Linear Change

Formula: Amount increases or decreases by a fixed increment each period (e.g., start at 100, add/subtract 10 each period).

Best for: Gradual phase-in or phase-out of rewards, smooth transitions between emission levels, controlled ramp-up periods.

Characteristics: Predictable rate of change, smooth supply curve transitions, good for temporary incentive programs.

Step Function

Formula: Different fixed amounts for different time periods (e.g., Year 1: 1000/day, Year 2: 500/day, Year 3: 250/day).

Best for: Complex tokenomics requiring different emission rates at different project phases, coordinating with roadmap milestones.

Characteristics: Maximum flexibility, can model any desired supply curve, useful for multi-phase projects.

Exponent Rate (m/n): Controls how rapidly exponential functions decrease (or increase). Smaller ratios = faster decay.

Divisor (d): Period length over which the exponent is applied, affecting decay speed.

Offset (o): Shifts the entire function up or down, useful for ensuring minimum emission values.

Base Amount (b): Starting amount before applying mathematical transformations.

Detailed Function Explanations

Fixed Amount Emission

Mathematical formula: emission(n) = constant

Parameters:

  • Amount: Fixed quantity released at each interval (e.g., 100 tokens)

Characteristics:

  • Supply grows linearly (straight line on supply chart)
  • Absolute inflation is constant, percentage inflation decreases over time
  • Simple to understand and communicate to users
  • Eventually becomes insignificant relative to total supply

Best practices: Ideal for simple staking rewards, loyalty programs, or situations where predictability is paramount. Ensure the fixed amount is sustainable long-term.

Exponential Decay Emission

Mathematical formula: emission(n) = a × (m/n)^(period/d) + o + b

Parameters:

  • Scaling Factor (a): Base multiplier (1-256). Higher values = more tokens initially.
  • Exponent Rate (m/n): Decay rate. m is numerator (-8 to 8, non-zero), n is denominator (1-32). Smaller ratios = faster decay. Common: m=1, n=2 for halving.
  • Divisor (d): Period length for exponent application. Larger values = slower decay.
  • Start Period Offset (s): Optional delay before decay begins (useful for initial fixed period).
  • Offset (o): Vertical shift (can be negative). Useful for ensuring minimum emissions.
  • Base Amount (b): Starting amount before transformations.
  • Minimum Value: Optional floor that emissions won't drop below.

Characteristics:

  • Rapid initial emissions that decrease over time
  • Asymptotic approach to maximum supply (never quite reaches but gets close)
  • Strong early-adopter incentives
  • Percentage inflation decreases exponentially

Best practices: Model carefully with spreadsheets before deployment. Bitcoin uses m=1, n=2, d=210000 blocks for its halving schedule. Start with conservative parameters and test thoroughly.

Linear Change Emission

Mathematical formula: emission(n) = initial + (slope × period)

Parameters:

  • Starting Amount: Initial emission quantity (e.g., 100 tokens)
  • Slope/Increment: Change per period (positive = increasing, negative = decreasing)
  • Duration: How many periods the linear change continues

Characteristics:

  • Predictable, steady change in emission rate
  • Smooth transitions between emission levels
  • Can ramp up (positive slope) or ramp down (negative slope)
  • Supply curve is parabolic (quadratic growth)

Best practices: Useful for temporary incentive programs with planned phase-out. Ensure the slope is appropriate for your timeline (too steep = abrupt changes, too shallow = prolonged adjustment).

Step Function Emission

Mathematical formula: emission(n) = amount_for_period(n)

Parameters:

  • Steps: Array of time periods, each with its own fixed emission amount
  • Period Boundaries: When each step begins and ends (by block, time, or epoch)
  • Amounts: Fixed emission for each period (can vary dramatically between steps)

Characteristics:

  • Maximum flexibility - can model any emission schedule
  • Abrupt transitions between emission rates
  • Can mimic any other function with sufficient steps
  • Supply curve has distinct phases (stepped appearance)

Best practices: Ideal for projects with clear roadmap phases. Define steps that align with project milestones (e.g., Year 1: Bootstrap, Year 2: Growth, Year 3: Maturity with corresponding emission rates).

History Tracking

Configure which token operations are permanently recorded on Dash Platform for transparency, compliance, and auditability. All flags default to TRUE.

Quick Reference

What is History Tracking?

What it is: History tracking creates permanent, queryable records of specific token operations stored directly on the blockchain. Each tracked operation includes timestamps, amounts, participants, and contextual data.

Why it matters: History tracking enables transparency, regulatory compliance, dispute resolution, and analytics. It allows token holders, auditors, and applications to query past events and verify the complete lifecycle of tokens.

Default behavior: All six history tracking flags default to TRUE, meaning all operations are tracked unless you explicitly disable specific types. This ensures maximum transparency by default.

Trade-off: More tracking provides better transparency and auditability but consumes more blockchain storage and increases transaction costs. Less tracking reduces costs and enhances privacy but limits forensic capabilities.

Trackable Operations (6 Flags)

1. Transfer History (keepsTransferHistory): Record when tokens move between addresses, including sender, receiver, amount, and timestamp. Supports encrypted notes for confidential transfer metadata (shared/private).

2. Freezing History (keepsFreezingHistory): Record when token balances are frozen or unfrozen, essential for compliance and security auditing.

3. Minting History (keepsMintingHistory): Record when new tokens are created, documenting supply expansion events with recipient and quantity details.

4. Burning History (keepsBurningHistory): Record when tokens are permanently destroyed, tracking supply reduction and the burning address.

5. Direct Pricing History (keepsDirectPricingHistory): Record when direct purchase pricing is modified, maintaining a historical price record for valuation analysis and marketplace transparency.

6. Direct Purchase History (keepsDirectPurchaseHistory): Record marketplace purchases where tokens are acquired directly from the contract owner (not peer-to-peer transfers).

Notes and Metadata

Transfer notes: The transfer operation supports encrypted notes up to 2048 characters. These can be shared (encrypted for both sender and receiver) or private (sender-only).

Character limit: All notes fields across token transitions have a maximum length of 2048 characters. This limit applies system-wide to maintain storage efficiency.

Encryption capability: Notes use identity-based encryption, enabling confidential metadata exchange while maintaining privacy for sensitive transaction details.

Implementation Considerations

Storage costs: Each tracked operation consumes blockchain storage. High-volume tokens should carefully select which operations to track to manage costs. Transfer tracking typically has the highest cost due to transaction frequency.

Privacy implications: History tracking makes operations permanently visible (though notes can be encrypted). Consider whether full transparency aligns with your token's privacy requirements.

Query performance: Tracked events are indexed and searchable, enabling efficient historical queries for wallets, explorers, and analytics tools.

Immutability: Once deployed, history tracking settings typically cannot be disabled (though they may be governable depending on your change control configuration). Choose carefully based on long-term requirements.

Token actions: Dash Platform supports 11 transition types (Burn, Mint, Transfer, Freeze, Unfreeze, Destroy Frozen Funds, Claim, Emergency Action, Config Update, Set Purchase Price, Purchase). History tracking determines which transitions are recorded.

  • High-security tokens: Disable if compromised accounts should be completely isolated

Important note: This parameter is currently not exposed in the wizard UI. To configure it, you'll need to edit the contract JSON directly or use advanced deployment methods. Consult Dash Platform documentation for implementation details.

Step-by-Step Configuration

  1. Define your launch strategy: Determine whether your token requires pre-activation verification or can launch immediately:
    • Immediate launch (not paused): Suitable for simple tokens with pre-tested configurations and no coordination requirements
    • Controlled launch (paused): Appropriate for complex tokens, high-value deployments, coordinated launches, or regulated assets
  2. Assess coordination needs: Evaluate whether you need to synchronize token activation with:
    • Marketing campaigns or public announcements
    • Exchange listings or marketplace integrations
    • Partner deployments or ecosystem launches
    • Regulatory approval or compliance milestones
    • Community voting or governance decisions
    • Distribution schedules or emission timing
  3. Evaluate risk tolerance: Consider the consequences of premature activation:
    • High-stakes or high-value tokens benefit from paused launches to ensure perfect execution
    • Regulated tokens may require paused launches to coordinate with compliance procedures
    • Simple utility tokens with low financial risk can launch active immediately
  4. Verify governance configuration: Before deciding to launch paused, confirm that:
    • You have configured who can unpause the token (via change control settings)
    • The unpause authority is appropriate for your governance model
    • You have secured the credentials/keys for the unpause authority
    • There's a backup plan if unpause authority is compromised or lost
  5. Decide on frozen account behavior: Determine the appropriate allowTransferToFrozenBalance setting:
    • Enable (true) if frozen accounts should receive dividends, distributions, or returns
    • Disable (false) if frozen accounts need complete isolation (regulatory/security)
    • Consider compliance requirements and whether partial suspension is acceptable
    • Note: This parameter requires direct JSON editing (not in wizard UI)
  6. Make your pause decision:
    • Enable "Start as Paused" if you need pre-activation verification, coordination time, or extra caution
    • Leave unchecked (default) if you're ready for immediate activation and have no coordination requirements
  7. Plan your activation (if paused): Document when and how you'll unpause the token, who will execute the unpause transaction, and how you'll communicate activation to stakeholders.
  8. Document frozen account policy: If using freeze capabilities, document when and how allowTransferToFrozenBalance applies, and communicate frozen account behavior to token holders.

Best Practices Checklist

  • Default to paused for high-value tokens: Securities, regulated assets, and high-financial-value tokens should almost always launch paused for maximum control and verification
  • Consider frozen account needs early: Decide on allowTransferToFrozenBalance setting based on whether frozen accounts should receive dividends, distributions, or returns
  • Test on testnet first: Whether launching paused or active, thoroughly test your exact configuration (including frozen account behavior) on testnet before mainnet deployment
  • Verify unpause authority before deploying paused: Confirm you control the identity/keys that can unpause the token. Losing unpause authority creates a permanently frozen token
  • Document your launch plan: For paused launches, create a written checklist of verification steps, activation procedures, and stakeholder communications
  • Document frozen account policy: If using freeze capabilities, clearly document when accounts will be frozen and how allowTransferToFrozenBalance affects them
  • Coordinate with partners before unpausing: Ensure wallets, explorers, exchanges, and integrators are ready before activating the token
  • Communicate launch status clearly: If launching paused, proactively communicate to token holders and community that the token is inactive and when activation is planned
  • Set activation expectations: Provide a concrete timeline or conditions for unpausing. Avoid indefinite paused states that create uncertainty
  • Have a rollback plan: Understand what happens if deployment fails or configuration is wrong. Can you pause again? What are the recovery mechanisms?
  • Monitor post-activation: Whether paused or active launch, monitor token operations closely after activation to catch and address issues quickly
  • Consider time zones and availability: Schedule unpause for a time when your team is available to monitor and respond to issues
  • Test freeze scenarios: If using freeze capabilities, test both freezing FROM and TO behavior on testnet to ensure allowTransferToFrozenBalance works as expected

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Losing unpause authority: Deploying paused without securing the unpause credentials creates a permanently frozen token. Back up keys and document unpause procedures
  • Wrong frozen account setting: Enabling allowTransferToFrozenBalance for high-security tokens or disabling it for tokens that need dividend distribution to frozen shareholders
  • Launching active without testing: Skipping paused launch for a complex or high-value token to save time, then discovering configuration errors after tokens are in circulation
  • No communication plan: Launching paused without telling anyone, causing confusion about why the token isn't working
  • Indefinite paused state: Deploying paused with no concrete activation plan, leaving token holders uncertain about when the token will become operational
  • Wrong unpause authority: Configuring unpause permissions incorrectly, requiring governance changes before the token can be activated
  • Unpausing prematurely: Activating before verification is complete, integrations are ready, or stakeholders are coordinated
  • No monitoring after activation: Unpausing and walking away without monitoring initial operations and user experience
  • Forgetting distribution schedules: Not considering how paused state affects automatic distributions or emission schedules
  • Not testing freeze behavior: Deploying with freeze capabilities but not testing how allowTransferToFrozenBalance affects frozen accounts on testnet
  • Inadequate testing: Testing paused launch on testnet but not testing the unpause operation itself
  • Poor timing: Unpausing during low-availability periods (weekends, holidays) when support and monitoring are limited
  • Forgetting to configure allowTransferToFrozenBalance: Not setting this parameter (requires JSON editing) and discovering the wrong behavior after deployment

Decision Framework: Should You Launch Paused?

Launch paused if any of these apply:

  • Your token represents significant financial value or is a regulated security
  • You need to coordinate activation with external events, announcements, or partners
  • You want time to verify deployment before enabling operations
  • Your token has complex configurations that benefit from post-deployment validation
  • Regulatory or compliance requirements dictate controlled activation
  • Community governance requires voting on activation timing
  • You're integrating with multiple services that need synchronization
  • This is your first production token deployment and you want extra caution

Launch active if all of these apply:

  • Your token has been thoroughly tested on testnet with identical configuration
  • No coordination with external parties is required
  • The token represents low to moderate financial value with manageable risk
  • Configuration is simple and well-understood
  • Immediate utility and activation is desired
  • You're comfortable with deployment risk and have recovery plans

When in doubt: Default to paused launch. You can always unpause quickly, but you cannot pause an actively-deployed token (unless pause functionality is specifically enabled in your configuration).

Security and Governance Considerations

Unpause authority security: Protect unpause credentials with the same rigor as token owner keys. Consider multi-signature schemes for high-value tokens.

Governance alignment: Ensure unpause authority aligns with your overall governance model. If the token is community-governed, consider committee or multi-sig unpause mechanisms.

Emergency procedures: Plan for scenarios where unpause authority is compromised, lost, or unavailable. Understand your token's governance recovery mechanisms.

Frozen account policy: The allowTransferToFrozenBalance setting has permanent implications. Coordinate with legal and compliance teams to ensure the setting aligns with regulatory requirements and token holder expectations.

Time-lock considerations: Some tokens implement time-locks where unpausing requires advance notice or delay. Understand if your token configuration includes such mechanisms.

Audit trail: Document the unpause transaction, who executed it, when, and why. This creates accountability and historical record for governance. Also document frozen account policy decisions.

Distribution interactions: If your token has automatic distributions, understand whether they execute while paused or only after unpausing. Also understand how distributions interact with frozen accounts based on your allowTransferToFrozenBalance setting.

Re-pause capability: Check whether your token can be re-paused after initial unpausing. Not all configurations support this. Plan for permanent activation if re-pausing is unavailable.

Compliance coordination: For tokens with freeze capabilities, ensure your allowTransferToFrozenBalance setting satisfies regulatory requirements. Some jurisdictions may require complete account isolation (false), while others may mandate continued dividend receipt (true).

Register Your Token

              
The final step: deploy your token contract to Dash Platform. This page guides you through choosing a registration method and completing the deployment process.


              

                
Quick Reference


                
What is Registration?

                
What it is: Registration is the process of deploying your token contract to the Dash Platform blockchain. It involves creating a blockchain identity (if needed) and submitting your contract as an immutable data structure.

                
Why it matters: Once registered, your token becomes a permanent part of the blockchain. The contract cannot be altered unless you explicitly configured change permissions in the Advanced step. Registration requires platform credits and network confirmations.

                
Key concepts:

                
    
                  
  • Identity: A unique identifier on Dash Platform that owns your contract
    • 
                  
  • Platform Credits: Network currency required to register identities and submit contracts
    • 
                  
  • Network: Testnet (for testing) or Mainnet (production)
    • 
                  
  • Immutability: Contracts cannot be changed after deployment (except via governance rules you defined)
    • 
                


                
Registration Methods

                
Mobile App (QR Code): The recommended method for most users. The wizard generates animated QR codes containing your complete token configuration. You scan these codes with the Dash mobile wallet app, which automatically handles identity creation, funding, and contract submission.

                
Use when: You prefer a mobile-first workflow, want the simplest experience, and don't want to expose sensitive keys to a web browser.

                
Dash Evo Tool (DET): A command-line interface for developers and advanced users. The wizard generates a downloadable contract JSON file with step-by-step CLI instructions for installation and deployment.

                
Use when: You need programmatic deployment, want to integrate with CI/CD pipelines, require audit trails, or prefer working with command-line tools.


                
Network Selection

                
Testnet: A sandbox environment for testing. Use testnet to experiment with your token configuration, verify behavior, and test integrations without spending real funds or creating permanent contracts.

                
Mainnet: The production Dash blockchain. Contracts deployed to mainnet are permanent and public. Only use mainnet when you've thoroughly tested your configuration and are ready for a live deployment.

                
Best practice: Always test on testnet first. Once you've confirmed everything works as expected, you can re-run the wizard and deploy to mainnet.


                
Costs and Requirements

                
Token Registration Fee: Registering a token on Dash Platform costs 0.1 DASH. This is a one-time fee that makes your token contract permanent on the blockchain.

                
Optional Keyword Fees: You can add up to 50 keywords for token discovery (3-50 characters each). Each keyword costs 0.1 DASH. Keywords are optional but help users find your token.

                
Distribution Fees: Perpetual distribution costs 0.1 DASH if enabled. Pre-programmed distribution costs 0.1 DASH if enabled. These are one-time fees for distribution features.

                
Example Total Costs: Basic token (no keywords, no distribution): 0.1 DASH. Token with 5 keywords and perpetual distribution: 0.8 DASH (0.1 base + 0.5 keywords + 0.1 distribution + 0.1 identity creation).

                
Token ID Generation: Your token ID is automatically generated by the platform using double SHA256 hash of: tokenContractId + tokenContractPosition + 'dash_token' string. You don't need to specify this.

                
Wallet Requirements: For self-service registration, you need a funded Dash wallet with a 12-word mnemonic phrase. For mobile/DET methods, the external tool handles wallet management.


                
The Deployment Process

                
Step 1 - Identity Check: The wizard checks if you already have a Dash Platform identity. If not, it creates one using your wallet.

                
Step 2 - Contract Generation: Your wizard configuration is converted into a valid Dash Platform data contract JSON payload.

                
Step 3 - Contract Submission: The contract is broadcast to the network and awaits confirmation by masternodes.

                
Step 4 - Confirmation: Once confirmed (typically 2-5 minutes), your token is live and ready to use. You'll receive a contract ID that uniquely identifies your token.


                
Security Considerations

                
Never share your mnemonic: Your 12-word recovery phrase provides complete control over your wallet and identity. Never enter it on untrusted websites or share it with anyone.

                
Self-service risks: The self-service method requires entering your mnemonic into this web application. While the wizard doesn't store or transmit your keys, browser-based key entry carries inherent risks. Only use this method on a trusted device with a test wallet.

                
Mobile/DET are safer: External tools (mobile app, DET) keep your keys isolated from the browser environment, reducing attack surface.


                
Technical Constraints

                
Keywords: Maximum 50 keywords allowed. Each keyword must be 3-50 characters in length. Keywords are optional and help with token discovery.

                
Description: Token descriptions must be 3-100 characters in length. Keep descriptions concise and informative.

                
Tokens Per Contract: Unlimited tokens per contract (only constrained by contract size). You can deploy multiple tokens within size limits.

                
Token Transition Fields: The platform automatically manages $identityContractNonce (64-bit), $tokenContractPosition (16-bit), $dataContractId (32 bytes), and $tokenId (32 bytes).

                
Epoch Timing: Masternode rewards are distributed approximately every 18 days (one Epoch on Dash Platform).


                
After Deployment

                
Contract ID: Your token is identified by a unique contract ID. Save this ID—you'll need it to interact with your token programmatically.

                
Testing: After deployment, test basic operations (transfers, minting if enabled, etc.) to verify everything works as expected.

                
Documentation: Document your contract ID, governance rules, and any change control processes for your team and community.

                
Monitoring: Use Dash Platform explorers to monitor your token's activity, supply changes, and transaction history.

Fee Calculation and Budget Planning

                  
Understanding the exact costs for token deployment helps you budget appropriately and avoid insufficient balance errors.


                  
Base Fees (Required):

                  
    
                    
  • Token Registration: 0.1 DASH (one-time, required)
    • 
                    
  • Identity Creation: ~0.1 DASH (one-time, if you don't have an identity)
    • 
                  


                  
Optional Fees:

                  
    
                    
  • Keywords: 0.1 DASH per keyword (maximum 50 keywords, 3-50 characters each)
    • 
                    
  • Perpetual Distribution: 0.1 DASH (if enabled in Distribution step)
    • 
                    
  • Pre-programmed Distribution: 0.1 DASH (if enabled in Distribution step)
    • 
                  


                  
Example Calculations:

                  
    
                    
  • Basic Token (no extras): 0.2 DASH (0.1 identity + 0.1 token)
    • 
                    
  • Token with 5 keywords: 0.7 DASH (0.1 identity + 0.1 token + 0.5 keywords)
    • 
                    
  • Token with 10 keywords + perpetual distribution: 1.3 DASH (0.1 identity + 0.1 token + 1.0 keywords + 0.1 distribution)
    • 
                    
  • Maximum configuration (50 keywords + both distributions): 5.4 DASH (0.1 identity + 0.1 token + 5.0 keywords + 0.2 distributions)
    • 
                  


                  
Budget Planning Tips:

                  
    
                    
  • Always add 10-20% buffer to your calculated total for transaction fees and network variations
    • 
                    
  • Check if you already have a Dash Platform identity to save 0.1 DASH
    • 
                    
  • Consider whether keywords are necessary for your use case—they add cost but improve discoverability
    • 
                    
  • Test on testnet first using free test credits before committing mainnet funds
    •

Use case: You're testing your token configuration for the first time

                  
    
                    
  • Method: Mobile or DET
    • 
                    
  • Network: Testnet
    • 
                    
  • Wallet: Create a new testnet wallet (don't use your mainnet wallet)

  • Estimated Cost: 0.2-0.5 DASH (0.1 identity + 0.1 token + optional keywords/distribution) using free testnet credits

  • Process: Fund testnet wallet from faucet → Select testnet in wizard → Deploy via preferred method → Test all token operations (transfers, minting, etc.) → Note your auto-generated token ID (SHA256 hash)

Scenario 2: Production Mainnet Deployment

                  
Use case: You've tested thoroughly and are ready for live production deployment

                  
    
                    
  • Method: Mobile (safest) or DET (if you need CI/CD integration)
    • 
                    
  • Network: Mainnet
    • 
                    
  • Wallet: Well-funded mainnet wallet with secure backup
    • 
                    
  • Estimated Cost: 0.2 DASH (basic) to 5.4 DASH (maximum with 50 keywords + distributions). Calculate: 0.1 (identity if needed) + 0.1 (token) + 0.1 per keyword + 0.1 per distribution feature + 20% buffer
    • 
                    
  • Process: Complete full testnet testing → Review all configuration one final time → Calculate exact deployment cost → Double-check network is set to mainnet → Deploy via preferred method → Save auto-generated token ID → Document contract ID and governance procedures → Announce to community/team
    • 
                    
  • Why: Mainnet contracts are permanent. Thorough testing and review minimize the risk of configuration errors. Token ID is auto-generated via SHA256 hashing.

Use case: You're integrating token deployment into automated workflows

  • Why: Multi-stage testing catches configuration issues before they reach production and provides confidence in your token's behavior. Each deployment gets unique auto-generated token ID.

Advanced Topics


                  
Multiple Tokens Per Contract:

                  
Dash Platform allows unlimited tokens per contract, constrained only by contract size. This enables efficient deployment of related token families.

                  
    
                    
  • Benefits: Deploy multiple related tokens (e.g., governance token + utility token) in a single contract to save on deployment fees and maintain logical grouping
    • 
                    
  • Considerations: Each token still requires its own registration fee (0.1 DASH) plus keyword/distribution fees. Total contract size must remain within platform limits
    • 
                    
  • Token ID Assignment: Each token gets a unique auto-generated token ID via SHA256 hash: tokenContractId + tokenContractPosition + 'dash_token'
    • 
                    
  • Contract Position: Tokens are identified by their position (16-bit unsigned integer) within the contract, starting at 0
    • 
                  


                  
Understanding Token Transition Fields:

                  
The platform automatically manages several fields during token operations. You don't need to specify these manually:

                  
    
                    
  • $identityContractNonce: 64-bit unsigned integer tracking identity's contract operations
    • 
                    
  • $tokenContractPosition: 16-bit unsigned integer indicating token's position in contract (0-65535)
    • 
                    
  • $dataContractId: 32-byte identifier for the data contract containing the token
    • 
                    
  • $tokenId: 32-byte auto-generated identifier via double SHA256 hash
    • 
                  


                  
Epoch-Based Operations:

                  
Some token operations are synchronized with Dash Platform's epoch cycle (~18 days). Understanding epochs helps with distribution timing:

                  
    
                    
  • Masternode Rewards: Distributed approximately every 18 days at epoch boundaries
    • 
                    
  • Distribution Cadence: If using epoch-based distribution in your token, emissions occur at these intervals
    • 
                    
  • Planning: Consider epoch timing when planning token launches and distribution schedules
    • 
                  

                


                

                  
Security Best Practices

                  
    
                    
  • Use testnet first, always: Never deploy to mainnet without thorough testnet testing. Contracts are immutable once deployed.
    • 
                    
  • Protect your mnemonic phrase: Your 12-word recovery phrase is the master key to your identity and token. Store it securely offline (paper wallet, hardware wallet, encrypted storage).
    • 
                    
  • Never reuse wallets: Use separate wallets for testnet and mainnet. Don't mix test and production credentials.
    • 
                    
  • Prefer mobile/DET over self-service: Browser-based key entry (self-service method) increases risk of keyloggers, XSS attacks, and browser extensions capturing your mnemonic.
    • 
                    
  • Verify contract ID after deployment: Immediately after successful deployment, verify your contract ID is recorded correctly. Losing this ID means you lose the ability to manage your token.
    • 
                    
  • Document governance procedures: If you enabled change controls, document who has permission to modify the contract and under what conditions.
    • 
                    
  • Use hardware wallets for mainnet: For production mainnet deployments holding significant value, use a hardware wallet (Ledger, Trezor) for maximum security.
    • 
                    
  • Audit before deployment: Have a second person review your contract configuration before mainnet deployment. Fresh eyes catch mistakes.
    • 
                    
  • Plan for key rotation: If using multi-signature governance, establish procedures for rotating keys and updating signer lists.
    • 
                    
  • Monitor after deployment: Set up monitoring for your token's activity. Watch for unexpected behavior, supply changes, or governance actions.
    •

    2.

    3.

    4.

      • 
                        
    • Network (testnet/mainnet)

      • 
                        
    • Token supply schedule and distribution rules
      • 
                        
    • Any change control processes you configured
      • 
                      
    
                    
    5. 
                    
  5. Set Up Monitoring: Configure alerts for key events (large transfers, minting actions, governance changes)
    6. 
                    
  6. Announce to Stakeholders: Inform your team, community, or users that the token is live. Provide contract ID and instructions for interaction.
    7. 
                    
  7. Plan for Operations: Establish operational procedures for day-to-day token management (if applicable), including who handles what responsibilities.
    8. 
                    
  8. Backup Everything: Store a complete backup of your wizard configuration, contract JSON, and deployment artifacts in secure, redundant storage.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

                  
    
                    
  • Deploying to mainnet without testnet testing: Always test on testnet first. Contracts cannot be deleted or easily modified after deployment.
    • 
                    
  • Insufficient wallet balance: Ensure you have enough funds to cover identity creation and contract submission. Running out of credits mid-deployment can cause failures.
    • 
                    
  • Wrong network selection: Double-check you're deploying to the intended network. Testnet contracts can't be moved to mainnet and vice versa.
    • 
                    
  • Not saving contract ID: Failing to record your contract ID immediately after deployment makes it difficult to manage your token later.
    • 
                    
  • Entering mnemonic on untrusted devices: Only use the self-service method on trusted devices with known security posture. Prefer mobile/DET methods.
    • 
                    
  • Skipping configuration review: Not reviewing all wizard steps before deployment can result in deploying incorrect settings (wrong supply, missing permissions, etc.)
    • 
                    
  • No backup strategy: Not backing up your wizard configuration and contract JSON means you can't easily redeploy or analyze what you deployed.
    • 
                    
  • Forgetting governance setup: Not documenting who controls what after deployment leads to confusion when changes are needed.
    • 
                    
  • Ignoring contract preview: Not using the "Contract Preview" button to verify your final configuration before deployment.
    • 
                    
  • Deploying with unclear distribution rules: Not understanding how your distribution/emission settings work can lead to unexpected token supply behavior.

Troubleshooting Guide


                  
Problem: "Insufficient Balance" Error

                  
    
                    
  • Cause: Wallet doesn't have enough Dash to cover all deployment fees
    • 
                    
  • Solution: Calculate your exact cost: 0.1 DASH (identity if needed) + 0.1 DASH (token) + 0.1 DASH per keyword + 0.1 DASH per distribution feature. Add 20% buffer. Example: Token with 10 keywords needs at least 1.4 DASH (1.2 + 20% buffer). Check balance using wallet app or DET tool.
    • 
                  


                  
Problem: "Identity Already Exists" Error

                  
    
                    
  • Cause: Your wallet already has a registered identity
    • 
                    
  • Solution: This is normal. The tool will use your existing identity to deploy the contract. No action needed.
    • 
                  


                  
Problem: "Contract Validation Failed" Error

                  
    
                    
  • Cause: Contract JSON has invalid structure or violates Dash Platform rules
    • 
                    
  • Solution: Use the "Show JSON" preview to check for errors. Common issues: keywords exceeding 50 limit, keywords not 3-50 characters, description not 3-100 characters, invalid token transition fields. Verify all required fields are present. Re-run the wizard if necessary.
    • 
                  


                  
Problem: "Too Many Keywords" Error

                  
    
                    
  • Cause: You've added more than 50 keywords (maximum limit)
    • 
                    
  • Solution: Reduce keywords to 50 or fewer. Each keyword also costs 0.1 DASH, so fewer keywords also reduces your deployment cost.
    • 
                  


                  
Problem: "Invalid Description Length" Error

                  
    
                    
  • Cause: Token description is not between 3-100 characters
    • 
                    
  • Solution: Edit your description to be at least 3 characters and no more than 100 characters. Keep it concise and informative.
    • 
                  


                  
Problem: Transaction Timeout

                  
    
                    
  • Cause: Network congestion or masternode issues
    • 
                    
  • Solution: Wait 5-10 minutes and check if the transaction completed. If not, retry the deployment. Your funds are safe.
    • 
                  


                  
Problem: QR Codes Not Scanning (Mobile Method)

                  
    
                    
  • Cause: Poor lighting, camera focus issues, or screen brightness too low
    • 
                    
  • Solution: Increase screen brightness, improve lighting, hold phone steady, ensure camera has permission to access. Try moving closer or farther from screen.
    • 
                  


                  
Problem: DET Command Not Found

                  
    
                    
  • Cause: DET not installed or not in PATH
    • 
                    
  • Solution: Run npm install -g @dashevo/dashevo-tool again. Verify with dashevo-tool --version. Restart terminal if needed.
    • 
                  


                  
Problem: Wrong Network After Deployment

                  
    
                    
  • Cause: Deployed to testnet when you meant mainnet (or vice versa)
    • 
                    
  • Solution: Unfortunately, contracts cannot be migrated between networks. You must redeploy to the correct network.
    •
